Description of British Library, MS Cotton Vespasian E.xvi (V)

Date: Mid-fifteenth century
Support: Parchment
Extent: 48 folios (bound with a group of 37 unrelated folios)
Format: 210-215 x 150-155mm
Foliation: The book comprises the three codicologically unrelated groups of leaves, two of which are found in fols. 1-37 and the remainder in fols. 38-85; only the latter are considered in this description. Foliation is continuous across these three units, however, commencing on the first parchment leaf and continuing throughout. Fol. 5 is repeated with an asterisk beside the second 5, which occurs on a blank leaf. Pairs of blank parchment leaves following fols. 25, 37, and 69 are not numbered; these were added by the binder. Fols. 1-5 in modern hand in pencil, and an earlier foliation which began on what is now 2r has been lined through; the doubling of fol. 5 permits the usage of an earlier foliation in ink, which runs throughout consecutively but appears to be in several hands. Fols. 62-77 were once numbered 70-85, while fols. 78-85 were once numbered 62-69, with the older numbers now crossed through, which suggests that the final two quires were previously bound in reverse order. The evidence of the quire signatures and the missing lines of Siege of Jerusalem, however, suggest that quire VI would not have come before quire V when the manuscript was first produced; apparently the quires were previously bound out of order, a mistake corrected in a later rebinding.
Collation:
I 38-45
II 46-53
III 54-61
IV 62-69
V 70-77
VI 78-85
Each quire is sewn through the center to a stub in the modern binding. Quire marks suggest that there were formerly two quires between what are now the fourth and fifth quires. These must have contained the beginning of Siege of Jerusalem; at about 30 lines per leaf, there would be 480 lines per quire, thus 960 in the two quires, which, with the 362 extant lines, would amount to a full copy of the poem. Quires are numbered x-xiii on leaves 38r, 46r, 54r, and 62r, and 70r is numbered xvi. 45v (i.e., the end of quire I) is labeled a, with 46r, 54r, and 62r labeled b, c, and d. A single catchword survives at 53v.
Condition: Parchment somewhat worn, but in sound condition.
Page layout: Siege of Jerusalem bounded in lead, with rulings visible on 70r but faded or never present elsewhere. Writing area in SJ 150-155 x 105 mm, with 29-36 lines per page. Similar, rather minimal layout for other texts, although The Three Kings of Cologne is bounded in brown crayon. Usually 29-31 lines per leaf for all texts. No prickings.
The scribe — script and dialect: Previous scholars have disagreed on the number of scribes and the distribution of their work. Guddat-Figge lists four scribes, with the first copying fols. 38r-69v, the second fols. 70r-75v, the third fols. 76r-82v, and the fourth fols. 83r-85r (179). Hanna-Lawton agree that there were four scribes, but suggest that each section is by “a different hand and the two items of the second booklet different hands” (xvii). The manuscript was also consulted by the editors of LALME (LP 553), who state that there is only one hand throughout (422).

I argue conversely that there are two scribes. Scribe 1 copied fols. 38-69 using dark ink and an anglicana script mixed with secretary forms. Scribe 2 copied fols. 70-85 in brown ink and a secretary hand. A side-by-side analysis of English words occurring in Siege of Jerusalem as well as in item 5, the prose treatise on physiognomy, is useful for confirming that the same hand is responsible for both. For example, And on 70r and 83r
I, moche on 72v and 83r
I, þ[er]fore on 72r and 83r
I, and wołł on 75r and 83r. While a word-to-word comparison cannot be made between SJ and the Latin texts in fols. 76r-82v, a comparison of letterforms indicates that these texts are by the same scribe who copied the English texts that bookend them. For example, the roman numeral xxx on 75r and 82v
I, the distinctive capital T
I and capital D
I found throughout, as well as the distinctive flat-topped capital N
I.

As Hanna-Lawton note, the fact that the scribe responsible for The Three Kings of Cologne is not the same as the scribe who copied Siege of Jerusalem means that we cannot apply the conclusions that the editors of LALME draw to SJ. While noting that the scribe of Siege does share agreement with LALME LP 553 in some instances, they also list a number of dialect features that point not to Oakham, Rutland, as LALME posits, but further east near Lincoln. They raise the possibility that “LALME’s LP 553 may show some interference from the Three Kings exemplar” (xvii). Kellogg's analysis of the manuscript's language, which focused exclusively on the SJ portion of the text, also attributes the scribe to the east Midlands, although he does not offer a more specifc locality. He does, however, provide a list of thirty dialect features, some of which, such as the -and present participle termination mentioned by Hanna-Lawton, make it clear that the dialect is distinct from that of Three Kings of Cologne. Hanna-Lawton also note the presence of the register of charters pertaining to Lincoln Cathedral in the front of the book; while this is not codicologically related to the portion containing Three Kings or Siege, it might be a further sign “pointing to such an east Midland provenance” (xvii).

Scribe 2 uses a variety of typical suspensions and abbreviations. The text is not heavily corrected, although there are examples of overwriting, erasure, and deletion, all of which appear to be the work of the original scribe and are handled according to the policy discussed in the general introduction. In addition, he has a number of peculiar or problematic tendencies that are worth noting, some of which create problems of interpretation or consistency in transcriptional policy:

Curl on -r: In nine instances, the scribe places a curl on final -r. In most cases, it is apparent that this is intended to be expanded to -re, as in her[e], l. 43
I, which is elsewhere spelled here, and tresour[e], l. 51, which is elsewhere spelled tresoure. In three cases – brothir, l. 29, and copper and silvir, both at l. 300 – the curl seems merely to be a flourish, as it does not curve back as far as the other six examples and the spelling does not require an expansion to -re. Thus these three have been transcribed -r'.

Barred letters: Barred letters, which tend to be otiose throughout most of the SJ corpus, are used by this scribe to indicate suspended -e, and have been expanded in the text accordingly. Note, e.g., barred wałł 1x vs. walle elsewhere, and tille vs. barred tiłł. Note also higħ vs. highe.

Otiose tildes: The scribe frequently uses an otiose tilde, as at worship, l. 19
I, come, l. 75, and wrought, l. 154. He is especially prone to place this mark above the letter p, as he does with worship at l. 19, vp, l. 30, and hap, l. 42.

Decoration: No marginalia appear alongside Siege of Jerusalem other than Septim[us] passus, which is the only surviving passus division and is written in the right margin of fol. 72r at a point that corresponds to a passus division and/or multiline capital in all of the other SJ manuscripts. Blue multiline capitals, boxed and decorated with red leaves and vines, appear at lines 60 and 148. The former corresponds to a large, if not multiline, capital in C, as discussed in the description of that manuscript; the latter corresponds to the passus division on 72r.

Three Kings of Cologne features similar 3-line blue lombards with red flourishing at section heads, and some proper names as well as Latin passages are framed in scribal ink. Item 3 is adorned with a single 4-line blue lombard with red flourishing. No decoration in items 4 and 5.

Binding: A British Library binding dated 1978 on the inside back cover. Binding measures 229 mm in height, 202 mm from center of spine to fore edge. Half bound, red morocco spine and corners over reddish brown bookboard. Blind-tooled double fillets on edges of leather, including leather corners, where they join the bookboard. Green and gold headband and tailband. A pair of thin gold fillets runs vertically on each side of the spine, and a pair of these lines defines the top and bottom of each compartment. The bottom compartment, which is larger than the others, features an additional pair of gold-tooled lines near the bottom of the spine so that it is split into two sections, one similar in size to the other compartments, and the other an additional area about 10 mm in height defined by the extra tooled lines. Compartments 1, 5, and 6 decorated with gold tools. Compartments 3, 4, and 5 have text in gold on black leather background:

Compartment 3: CHARTULARIUM / LINCOLNIENSE

Compartment 4: BRITISH / LIBRARY

Compartment 5: COTTON / MS / VESPASIAN / E. XVI

Front and back covers have gold stamp of Cotton's coat of arms in center.

A white oval sticker at the top of the spine reads 684; another at the bottom of the spine reads b. Stamped in black ink, with a black ink frame, on rear pastedown: B.L. 1978. On front pastedown is written 684 B. A slip of paper affixed to the verso of the second paper flyleaf in the rear notes that the manuscript was "examined after Binding" in 1978. In the front, a modern paper pastedown and four modern paper flyleaves. This is followed by a single earlier paper flyleaf from a previous binding, and this by a parchment leaf (fol. 1 in the present foliation) that may also have been a flyleaf in a previous binding. In the rear, a single paper flyleaf on early paper matching that in the front, followed by three modern paper flyleaves and a modern paper pastedown. A modern parchment bifolium is found following fols. 25, 37, and 69. The outer edges of leaves towards the end of the volume appear singed, which likely happened during the fire at the Ashburnham House in 1731.

Contents:
Fols. 1-37 contain two groups of leaves: fols. 2-25, a 13th-century register of charters pertaining to Lincoln cathedral, and fols. 26-37, a 16th-century Latin chronicle of Lichfield. These are codicologically unrelated to the unit of 48 folios containing The Siege of Jerusalem.
1. Fols. 38r-69v The Three Kings of Cologne IPMEP 290. Ends imperfectly.
2. Fols. 70r-75v The Siege of Jerusalem IMEV 1583. Begins imperfectly at Hanna-Lawton l. 966
3. Fols. 76r-77v Latin prose treatise on the calendar, incipit Cum in vnaquaue sciencia est vnum generale in quod resoluuntur omnia que sunt. Ends imperfectly.
4. Fols. 78r-82v Latin prose medical treatise, incipit brachium et ideo puslus sinistro brachio
5. Fols. 83r-85r Middle English prose treatise on physiognomy, incipit Among all þinges who þat woll knowe the mervelous science of ffysnomy
Provenance: Nothing is known of the manuscript's early history. An ex libris in the group of leaves comprising fols. 2-11, material unrelated to the manuscript containing Siege of Jerusalem, reads liber cartarum eccleise beate Marie Lincoliensis. As discussed above, it is impossible to know whether this has bearing on the origins of the portion of the book containing SJ. As a group, the famous Cotton manuscripts were owned by Sir Robert Bruce Cotton (1571-1631) and presented as a gift to the nation of Great Britain by his grandson, Sir John Cotton, in 1700. They were initially housed in Ashburnham House, Westminster, where a fire destroyed or damaged about twenty-five percent of them in 1731. The manuscripts are now part of The British Library.
