Date: On the basis of the handwriting, the manuscript seems to date to the second half of the fifteenth century.
Support: Paper
Extent: A portion of one folio
Format: A paper strip about 280 x 60 mm. Hanna-Lawton estimate that the original leaf would have measured approximately 325 x 275 mm.
Foliation: None
Collation: All that survives of this copy of The Siege of Jerusalem is this single strip of paper, which was discovered during restoration work on the memorandum book of Furse of Morshead, in which it had been incorporated as a binding strip. The discovery was publicized by Michael Swanton in 1990. The fragment contains only portions of thirty-four lines of text on the front and thirty-three on the obverse. The surviving lines correspond to Hanna-Lawton 985-1017 and 1106-1138; this gap of eighty-nine lines may not be the exact number missing in Ex, but must at least be quite close to the actual count (the fact that the text is presented in two columns makes this count higher than one would ordinarily expect from two sides of the same leaf). Because it is impossible to ascertain what the original line numbers would have been, the lines have been numbered beginning with line 1 and continuing consecutively even though this gap exists; as with other manuscripts, however, the lines are also keyed to Hanna-Lawton's line numbers, which reflect both the gap between the lines on the front and back of the leaf and the location of the text approximately 1000 lines into the poem. The front and back of the fragment, meanwhile, have been designated 1r and 1v.
Condition: Fragmentary
Page layout: Swanton notes that "[t]he writing-space is apparently complete at the lower edge, with a 2.5 cm bottom margin; the upper edge has been cropped however, with perhaps seven lines of writing cut away" (103). What survives of the manuscript suggests a careful presentation of the text in a codex with high production standards. Swanton suggests that it is "more grandly set out than any of the extant copies hitherto known" (103), although it was certainly rivaled, and perhaps exceeded, by U. The poem was originally copied in double columns, a practice unusual for poems written in the alliterative long line and occurring elsewhere in the surviving corpus of Siege of Jerusalem manuscripts only in P.
The scribe — script and dialect: The text is written in a clear anglicana script. There is no evidence of medial punctuation, but the cropping is so severe that no full half-lines survive, and thus it is not possible to see whether the caesura was marked. Although linguistic evidence is scant in a fragment of this size, Hanna-Lawton are able to offer some helpful observations about the dialect: "The fragment is far too brief for any definitive linguistic analysis, although it generally appears to be in a central north Midland dialect (Notts.? Derbys.?). The copyist has -es in the present third person singular but -en in the plural, o for OE ā, shall, them. One unusual form, 1006 thurw "through" (although at the edge of the fragment and conceivably the remains of thurwe or thurwh or thurwȝ) is recorded in LALME (3, 591-92, grid 472 456) as the majority form of but a single scribe, LP 228, from the upper Derwent valley just northeast of York City (perhaps Kirkham OSA?)."

Decoration: One rubricated paraph survives at what would have been Hanna-Lawton line 985.

Contents:
A fragment of The Siege of Jerusalem IMEV 1583 corresponding to Hanna-Lawton 985-1017 and 1106-1138.
Provenance: Nothing is known of the origins of the manuscript other than it was found in the memorandum book of Furse of Morshead, "a family from Kingsbridge, south Devon" (Hanna-Lawton xix).
